Roomba Robot NiCd Battery Modification

A friend gave me his old (original model) Roomba robotic vacuum cleaner some time back. He had lost the charger for it, but more importantly, the NiCd battery pack was really old and would no longer hold a charge - at least the way it used to. So I removed the original battery pack from the plastic casing.
I noted the dimensions of the OEM battery housing and planned to build up my own battery pack, from scratch. To meet the main DC voltage requirements for the Roomba, I needed 4-series Lithium-ion cells (3V min, 3.7V nominal, 4.2V max - per cell), so totaling a range between 12V to 16.8V. Since I had enough room in the battery's plastic case, and I wanted to ensure the robot could vacuum my entire apartment (at least once), I went with 2 parallel cells per each series cell.

I soldered together eight individual 1320 mAh lithium-ion polymer battery cells in a 4-series and 2-parallel (4s2p) arrangement, which I placed into the old battery's housing. I rewired the old metal-contact that was being used for a temperature line to instead serve as a "system present" pin. The temperature pin was no longer needed, since the fuel-gauge IC had its own internal temperature monitoring system (two thermistors, with four-tier software and hardware safety features). I wired a three-way switch to toggle between OFF/ON/SLEEP, using the metal contact as a simple and direct method to put the fuel-gauge IC into a low-power sleep mode - which minimizes power losses when the robot is not in active use. The resultant battery pack is shown in this old casing (with its top removed to show the circuit board) when calibrating the fuel-gauge and running some (dis)charge cycle tests.
I used a COTS Li-ion polymer battery charger, and modified it for the charge plug I wanted to use. After minimal rewiring and modifications the robot work great! It last much longer than it ever did (over 2 hours), and has plenty of power to supply its motors.

Lithium-ion Polymer Battery Pack Fuel-Gauge PCBs

Two printed circuit boards (PCBs) are illustrated here, both are very similar, but are retrofitted for use in slightly different battery packs. The top shows a 2-series and 1-parallel (or 2s1p, for short) 1900 mAh lithium-ion polymer battery (LiPB) pack, whereas the bottom image shows a 1320 mAh 3s1p LiPB pack. In both pictures, you can see the finished PCB mounted on top of the battery pack (far left), the PCB fully populated but not attached to a battery (middle), and an unpopulated bare PCB (far right).
